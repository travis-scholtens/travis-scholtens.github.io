TrueSkill for Divisions

Travis Scholtens

Abstract

The TrueSkill and TrueSkill Through Time skill rating systems are generally useful in ranking players in a population by skill level by inferring skill levels from the outcomes of multiple games. But in cases where players compete in leagues separated into divisions by skill level, and primarily compete within their own division in a given season, the inferred skill levels are unreliable. High level players in one division may be incorrectly ranked alongside less competitive players in another, and as a consequence the model loses predictive accuracy, particularly in games between players from different division. By inferring skill independently among the players of each division, and then estimating skill differences across divisions, we improve upon the accuracy of both TrueSkill and TrueSkill Through Time in predicting the outcomes of future competitions.

Introduction

The TrueSkill skill rating system (Herbrich et al. 2006) represented a significant improvement over previous efforts, such as the Elo system used in chess, by using Bayesian inference to estimate skill levels rather than relying on heuristics. TrueSkill Through Time (Herbrich et al. 2007) built on this approach by allowing the outcomes of later competitions to influence the skill estimates at earlier times, and allowing for the evolution of players' skill levels over time.

Both of these systems produce skill estimates that are more successful than Elo ratings when predicting the outcomes of new games. But both systems' accuracies decrease when the matchups among players are not distributed uniformly.

In particular, when players are formally divided according to experience or skill, as is often done in recreational sports leagues, TrueSkill can fail to accurately capture the skill differences between players in different divisions.

If players never compete across divisions, then no global rating can be inferred without additional information. But in many real world organizations, interdivisional matchups are relatively rare but not uncommon, whether they take place in practice matches, open tournaments outside of league play, or due to substitutions when a player needs to temporarily "play up" to fill an open position in a higher division team's lineup.
